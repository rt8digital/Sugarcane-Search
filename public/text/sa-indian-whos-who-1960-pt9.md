They came in response to a request from the Natal Government for labour for the sugar planters. History records that the indentured labourers came at the express wish of the Natal Colonists. History also records that they played no small part in the building up of Natal. Indian indentured labour stopped in 1911, after the Government of India had expressed dissatisfaction at the treatment of the labourers. No person has more eloquently stated the historical facts than the late Jan H. Hofmeyr, then Deputy-Prime Minister of the Union, Minister of Finance and Education and Chancellor of the University of the Witwatersrand, when in 1945, in a Hoernle wlemorial Lecture, under the auspices of the South African Institute of Race Relations, he stated:— It is well that we should remind ourselves how the Indian came to South Africa. There has perhaps never been a better summing up of the attitude of the average European in South Africa to the Asiatic than that given by Lord Milner: “The Asiatics are strangers forcing themselves upon a community reluctant to receive them.” .. . it is an inescapable fact that the Asiatics came to South Africa primarily not of their own initiative, but because the Europeans wished them to come—they remained because the Europeans wished them to remain. So it was in the days of the Dutch East India Company when the Malays first came to the Cape; so it was in the nineteenth century with the coming of the Indians. If our history proves anything it is this —- that however we may regard our Asiatic problem, the fact that it came into existence is due to the Europeans alone. There was only one reason for the original introduction of immigrants from India to what is now the Union of South Africa -— the desire of the Natal Colonists of those days to exploit the potential wealth of their coastal districts. The Natives, so the sugar planters. declared, would not come out to work in sufficient numbers from the reserves which Sir Theophilus Shepstone had set aside for them, and when they did come their labour was inefficient. The nascent sugar industry was denied the hope of expansion if it could not get the labour it required. But in not-too-distant Mauritius Indian indentured para had set sugar planting on its feet. Why should not Natal follow suit— and prosper similarly ? So the Government of India was asked to sanction the importation of Indian coolies. At first it was unwilling — ultimately it agreed, and in November 1860 the first shipload arrived. They came at the expense of the Natal Government, which allocated them to approved masters under a three-years’ indenture. After the three years the labourer was required to re-indenture himself for a fourth year, or, if he wished, for two additional years. Thereafter he was free to live and work as he willed. After a further five years he had the right either to a return passage or the equivalent of its cost in Crown land. The ultimate end of this policy was clear from the outset. The coolie was to be welcomed as a permanent settler in the Colony, and a contributor to its prosperity. The conception of the Indian as a stranger forcing himself upon a reluctant community had not emerged. His coming amply justified the predictions of those who had favoured it. The sugar industry prospered mightily, and in the words of a leading South African historian, Sugar became King in Natal. When in 1886 the Government of India forbade importation of indentured labourers it was held to portend ruin for the Colony. With great difficulty the withdrawal of the ban was secured. Of course the coming of the Indian also brought disadvantages, which in due course asserted themselves. As a result the policy of Natal gradually changed, but the importation of indentured labour did not cease until 1911, and then it was the Government of India that stopped further recruiting. Indians in Natal had the vote until 1896, when they were disfranchised by the Natal Government by the Franchise Amendment Act. In 1935, there were 10, in 1939, four, in 1945 two, and in 1948 none. Indians in Natal were deprived of the municipal vote by an Ordinance of the Natal Provincial Council in 1924. Under Natal Municipal Law 19 of 1872, Indians who possessed the necessary property qualification, were entitled to vote in the election of Councillors. When the Ordinance of 1924 was challenged in the Court on the ground that it discriminated against Indians, the Court held that the Provincial Council was within its rights in passing the Ordinance. In the words of the learned Judge, “There is no limit to the right to discriminate ... Nor are we concerned with the propriety or expediency of such legislation.” Indians in the Cape Province were entitled to the vote under the Constitution Ordinance of 1853 provided they met the necessary property and salary qualifications. Two years ago, there were about 400 Indians on the voters rolls in the Cape. Indians in the Transvaal Republic enjoyed no franchise at any time; the same was the case with Indians in the Orange Free State. No more than a mere handful of Indians lived in the Free State at any given time. The South Africa Act, 1909, excluded all Indians from the franchise. The position today is that Indians have no say whatsoever in the Central, Provincial and Local Government bodies, although they pay taxes on the same basis as Europeans. The policy of the ruling Nationalist Party is against the granting of any franchise to Indians. According to its Election Manifesto, the Party will strive to “repatriate or remove elsewhere, as many Indians as possible, with the co-operation of India and of other countries.” There is no prospect of the Governments of India and Pakistan ever agreeing to the expatriation of Indians. The policy of the main Opposition Party, the United Party, does not differ in any material respect from that of the Nationalist Party. The United Party which, under General Smuts, passed the Asiatic Land Tenure and Indian Representation Act (1946) is today, under Sir de Villiers Graaff, no longer prepared to grant even the meagre communal representation which General Smuts granted as a “quid pro quo” for the land tenure provisions of the Act which came to be known as the “Ghetto Act”. The provisions in regard to representation were, however, never put into effect, and when the Nationalist Party came to power in 1948, it repealed the franchise provisions of the Act. One of the main stumbling blocks to the putting into effect of the Act was the fact that Indians of all political shades of opinion were unanimous in rejecting communal representation. The Act was however important as indicative of the maximum the Smuts Government — a United Party Government—was prepared to offer. In the 48 years since Union, the Act was the only concession any Government was prepared to make in respect of Indian representa- tion, and then only because of the towering personalities of Smuts and Hofmeyr who were able to carry their Party with them. Under the Act, Indians were to be represented by 3 members in the House of Assembly, 2 Senators and 2 members in the Natal Provincial Council. Natal and Transvaal were to be divided into 3 electoral divisions for the election by Indians, who were to be placed on a separate roll, of 3 M.P.s. One of the two Senators was to be nominated on the ground mainly of -his. thorough acquaintance by reason of his official experience or otherwise with the reasonable wants and wishes of the Indians of Natal and Transvaal. The other Senator was to be elected by the Indians of Natal and Transvaal. Natal was to be divided into two electoral divisions for the election of 2 Provincial Councillors to represent Indians. Suitably qualified Indians could stand for election as Provincial Councillors. This provision was the first which admitted any non-Europeans to a higher legislative body in the Union.
