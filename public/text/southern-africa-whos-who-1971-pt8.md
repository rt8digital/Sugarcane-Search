284 OF The present generations of the Ramprotals who live in and around Nonoti, the little settlement of Indian farmers on the North Coast, are the direct descendants of Sambal who arrived in this country in 1874. He was born in Arejilla in 1834. Sambal was a remarkable man for unlike other indentured Indians of the time he was soon in the retail trade. He owned two shops in the Blackburn and Shakaskraal areas and became asuccessful businessman in his own right. He first séttled in Blackburn and then moved to Mt. Edgecombe. Shortly after the cessation of hostilities bet- ween Briton and Boer, Sambal decided to move to Nonoti where he bought a hundred acre farm and allied with farming, continued with retail trading activities in the area. He had meanwhile disposed of the Blackburn and Shakaskraal businesses. He had married shortly after his arrival in this country and in 1884, Ramprotal, an only son, was born. At an age when most boys are at school, Ramprotal began helping on the farm and in the running of the business. The present shop and home were built in 1920 and here the family grew and prospered. By 1918, due to Ramprotals business acumen, the farmland totalled close on to six hundred acres. He married Joso- thiry Bodhoo in 1911. Ramprota! died on the 2nd of November, 1958. He was a respected and well liked figure on the local scene and his efforts towards the establishment of the Nonoti school and other religious and social activi- ties are indicative of the mans public spiritedness. He was probably the first Indian school grantee in Natal. The school became a Government-Aided School in 1920 and celebrated its golden jubilee in 1970. He was instrumental, amongst others, in forming the Lower Tugela Veda Dharma Sabha. He came here in a spirit of adventure and was not concerned with the low pay and bad conditions. Like many others he came to love the country and decided to settle here.
