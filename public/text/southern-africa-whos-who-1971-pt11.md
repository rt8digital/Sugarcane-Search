It is barely a decade since Indians became an accepted and permanent part of the South African population although the reasons for doing so were more inclined towards making the Indian question in this country an internal one, thus eliminating the U.N.O.’s yearly onslaught. In spite of this development, the Indians remained unaccepted and unwanted and a foreign, and so-called unassimilable element in local politics until fairly recently. The Group Areas and other discriminatory Acts reached absurd heights over the years. There were Proclamations and de-Proclamations. The sword of Democles seemingly hung over Indian heads in every field of activity. The “Winds of Change” in the late sixty’s however brought about change in internal thinking and this noticeable trend has happily continued into the seventy’s. This was brought about by continued and increasing South African isolation in every field of the world community’s activity and these pressures in- duced rethinking in racial attitudes. We see perhaps the death throes of Apartheid in this country as more pressure is applied internally and externally. We forsee an age when real lasting values, such as merit, intelligence and integrity, will play an ever-increasing part in the assessment of an individual’s worth. The overwhelming number of Indians who came here earlier and eventually settled in this country, were indentured labourers and they made their contribution to the country’s development, not only in the cane fields but also in the introduction of tobacco, tea, rice and other crops indigenous to India, Mauritius and the West Indies. How important was this contribution can be gauged by the fact that the young, fledgling colony became viable in the first decade of the Indian arrival and the Colony saw the first and many more of its exports leave its shores for a foreign port. The years preceding this had been bleak and the Colony had been facing bankruptcy in terms of national economy. Much has been written of the activities of the workers in the cane fields but little is known of the contribution of those Indians who arrived later in the century as traders. Of this section of the Indian population, little has been writeen and generally , very much less has been discussed of their contribution to the development of the country’s lines of communication and trade but facts are now emerging which prove conclusively that these intrepid pioneers were the first to open up many parts of the interior to trade and whole towns and villages sprouted round these early trail blazers before the turn of the century. In the Eastern Transvaal and Northern Natal, the Indian trader, in his ramshackle building, was the first to set up a trading post in mainly inaccessible parts of the country, transporting his goods by mule, horse and donkey pack and by ox-waggon. It was very similar to the exploits of the early trader in the Wild West who blazed a trail across the prairies and established communications and settlements round which grew great American towns and cities. First came the trader, followed by the usual number of allied hangers-on who supplied a need to the developing community such as the Farrier and Blacksmith, an inn, the first Green Grocer, the Postal Agent and the gradual tentative approach of the farmer and small-holder who desired to be nearer his source of supply of everyday needs, to be soon followed by another, and yet another. From the early years of the First World War and at an accelerated pace ircm the twenty’s, a number of Indians from both the trading and working class, broke away from their common pursuits and entered the manufacturing field. Many of these people were accomplished workmen and craftsmen, having learnt and mastered their particular following at home and once in South Africa, began to ply their trade once free of the shackles of indenture. They were builders, carpenters, metal workers, gold and silversmiths, tailors and leather workers and it was not really surprising when they Jaunched out into these fields so that by the tail-end of the thirty’s, many of them were established manufacturers of some repute. Today, the Indian has a more refined approach and he has become a first class manufacturer of General Clothing, Footwear, Furniture, Canned and Processed Foods, Metal goods and other products in Light Industry and has made in-roads into Light-Heavy Industry to a lesser extent. In the professions, the Indian has made tremendous progress in the Medical field, Nursing, other Health Services, Teaching and Law. In Technical Trades and at work in skilled and semi-skilled occupations he has given the impression of a sober, industrious, intelligent, responsible and dedicated worker. These qualities make him a much sought after worker but unfortunately, provincial barriers and other restrictions on the Indian make it impossible for an intelligent and fruitful use of his undoubted assets. The Indian, in this country, is well equipped to meet the challenge of the present age of constant change and flux and adaptability to new ideas and situations. The fanatical Indian dedication to education and the deep rooted moral upbringing, stemming no doubt from their inherent religious leanings, make for a more stable and responsible member of the South African social order. The Indian contribution to the common cause in this country has been considerable, out-weighing by far the contribution of any other community, in spite of its lack of opportunities in trade, industry and education. The Indian, strangely enough, owes much to Apartheid in an indirect sense. It has made him a better member of the general community. This pernicious doctrine, in its attempt to destroy the Indian, has had an opposite effect and instead, has made the Indian aware of the need for education; had made it necessary for him to acquire certain skills, and had made him more stable in his approach to everyday living. He has had to fight discrimination at every step and to rise above crippling disadvantages has not always been easy. Apartheid presented a challenge which he had to accept and the ‘damage to his dignity and self-respect, he never shows although by nature he is sensitive and deeply emotional. His abhorrence of it is shown in his constant struggle and opposition to this dehumanizing ideology. However, without Apartheid there would have been no worlds to conquer, no ambition to pursue. The opening up of fresh avenues in the Public Service sector, a hither-to closed door because of Job Reservation, is an encouraging sign. The Posts and Telegraphs, Railways, Police and other Government departmental openings augur well for the Indian youth. Statistics show that there are more than 300,000 children in the Indian group who are below the age of sixteen. It is this age group which will make such a tremendous impact on a future South Africa,unencumbered by archiac laws and stupid prejudice. The coming of T.V. and its attendant technological requirements, should make an added call on the initiative and intellectual capacity of Indians to a great extent. In fact in every field of activity, the Indian will be faced with further exciting challenges. He will no doubt meet these challenges just as his forebearers did, and who, to their everlasting credit, overcame them under conditions and circumstances vastly different from those of today. As long as the Indian remained a common labourer and was subservient to him, there was little objec- tion to his remaining here but no sooner he aspired to better things, the law and the people conspired to out him in his place. Subrati, one of a family of 16, began clothing manufacture on 2 treadle sewing machines in a small room of the house shortly after leaving the services of a Mrs. Hart near Albert Park where he had learnt the use of a sewing machine early in the 20’s. Within a year the first factory, a wood and iron structure, came into being and in 1921, the factory moved to Crabbe Street in Greyville to be closer to the firm’s best customer, J. Nunnerlaly and Company who supplied the cloth and sold and distributed the made-up clothing. At this stage the factory oper- ated 80 machines. By 1924 the factory had over 60 workers and had moved to larger premises in Raleigh Street. High overheads in the city financially affected the business to such an extent that the Standard Clothing, as it was then known, closed down and re-opened at the original wood and iron premises in Mayville and a year later moved into a concrete struc- ture, the wood and iron building becoming the office and stock room of the firm. This was burnt down during the 1949 riots when the business suffered a loss of over R16,000.00 from fire and looting. The change from treadle to electrically operated machines came about in 1936, The number of machines up to then numbered just over a dozen. Distance from the city centre and transport were a clear advantage and horse-drawn vehicles solved the means of trans- porting goods. By 1940 the factory employed over 60 workers and operated 40 machines, reaching its peak by 1950 with over 120 employees and as many machines. In 1949, S. A. Subrati became a partner in the firm established by A. K. Subrati and the name Mayville Clothing was adopted for the first time. A. K. Subrati died in February 1960, by which time the area had been declared White under the Group Areas Act of 1957, Operating under permit the Subrati family now runs the factory, and as tenants of the Group Areas Board, operate the factory until alternative premises are provided in terms of the Act, in some other area in the near future. Over the years the factory has earned a fine reputation in the manufacture of Khaki clothing and as ‘‘Khaki Kings” the title is well merited.
